Forecasting and Volume Estimation of Municipal Solid Waste Using Machine Learning

Aryan Goyal

B.Tech-M.Tech Dual Degree Project • IIT Bombay • December 2025

Supervisor: Prof. Munish K. Chandel

This thesis addresses two interconnected challenges in waste management: forecasting MSW generation using economic indicators across countries, and estimating waste volumes from single RGB images using monocular depth inference—together forming a closed-loop data-driven framework for waste monitoring.

Back Resume

The Problem: Waste Data Deficit

Effective solid waste management requires knowing how much waste is generated, where it originates, and what it comprises. These measurements underpin every downstream operation—from route optimization and segregation efficiency to treatment, recycling, and policy evaluation.

In developing countries like India, these challenges are compounded by systemic limitations: lean budgets, limited instrumentation (weighbridges, vehicle tracking), and significant inter-jurisdictional variability. Even when data exist, they often lack temporal consistency, spatial representativeness, and standardization across urban local bodies.

Two Complementary Research Directions:
1. Forecasting models that infer waste generation from stable socioeconomic variables where direct measurement is inconsistent
2. Low-cost volume estimation systems that operate on-site without expensive hardware, enabling continuous monitoring

Research Objectives

O1: Low-cost Volume Measurement

Engineer a controlled-condition vision pipeline that converts a single RGB image into a metric volume estimate over a known ROI (fixed pose, planar reference, calibrated footprint).

O2: Repeatability by Design

Guarantee repeatability via fixed camera geometry, explicit pixel-to-metric scaling, and tunable controls (ground-depth percentile, scale, threshold).

O3: Forecasting Framework

Build statistical and ML forecasters for MSW; determine which socioeconomic factors most strongly influence generation across economies.

O4: Cross-country Benchmarking

Benchmark India's waste efficiency against peers (China, USA, OECD) using intensity metrics and elasticity analyses.

Part 1: Forecasting MSW Generation

The forecasting component examines how efficiently countries generate waste relative to their economic growth. Using standardized cross-country datasets covering China, India, OECD, and the United States, the analysis evaluates MSW generation against indicators such as GDP, disposable income, urbanization, and retail spending.

Key Questions

  • Which growth factors show the strongest correlation with MSW per capita?

  • How efficiently do different economies convert growth into waste generation?

  • Is India's trajectory lagged relative to China's, or does it indicate a more efficient pattern?

  • Can mature economies (USA, OECD) serve as benchmarks for India's policy evolution?

China: Transition Toward Efficiency

China's MSW per-capita rose from approximately 0.31 to 0.50 kg/day between 2005–2025, tracking sustained growth in GDP, disposable income, and retail sales. Policy inflection points include the National Sword policy (2017–2018), nationwide waste-sorting mandates (from 2019), and incineration expansion under the 12th–14th Five-Year Plans.

China (2005–2025): Per-capita MSW and major economic indicators. The flattening toward 2025 aligns with slower population growth and early effects of prevention initiatives.

China: MSW per-capita vs. economic drivers (2005–2023). Strong positive correlations (r ≈ 0.94–0.97) reflect synchronized macro growth.

China Key Findings:
• Total MSW increased by ~41%, while per-capita rose by 29% (2005–2023)
• Elasticities: Income (0.275), Consumption (0.294), Retail (0.254) — all inelastic
• Urbanization elasticity remains high (1.435)
• Declining MSW per $1B of income confirms improving material efficiency

India: Volatile and Transitional

Between 2015–2021, India's per-capita MSW fluctuates between 0.08–0.12 kg/day with no consistent upward trend despite steady economic growth. Variability arises from inconsistent reporting across Urban Local Bodies, changing coverage, and differing inclusion of waste categories.

India (2015–2021): Per-capita MSW and economic indicators. The mild upward drift suggests expansion of formal collection rather than increased generation intensity.

India: MSW per-capita vs. selected drivers (2015–2021). Small samples (n ≤ 7) yield indicative but non-conclusive correlations.

India Data Challenges: The Swachh Bharat Mission (2014–) and Solid Waste Management Rules (2016) improved data coverage but introduced step-changes—discontinuities driven by administrative inclusion rather than real changes in waste generation.

OECD and USA: Mature Systems

OECD average (2005–2025): Gradual rise from 1.35 to 1.6 kg/day amid stable population and rising affluence.

USA (2005–2025): Per-capita MSW remains flat (2.0–2.15 kg/day) despite income growth—signifying partial decoupling.

OECD: High correlations (r ≈ 0.97) mirror broad-based expansions moderated by standardized data quality.

USA: Moderate correlations (r ≈ 0.52–0.66) confirm partial decoupling from income growth.

Part 2: Vision-Based Volume Estimation

While macro-level forecasting establishes broad efficiency trajectories, facilities require scalable methods to measure waste volumes on the ground. Traditional measurement systems—weighbridges, manual tallying, LiDAR scanning—are either capital-intensive or impractical for continuous deployment in resource-constrained settings.

We developed a low-cost, controlled vision pipeline that estimates waste volume from a single RGB image using monocular depth inference. The approach requires no LiDAR, stereo cameras, or complex calibration rigs—just a fixed viewpoint, calibrated footprint, and repeatable lighting conditions.

Why DINOv3?

DINOv3 is a state-of-the-art self-supervised visual transformer that learns rich spatial features through self-distillation. Its dense, high-resolution embeddings naturally align with scene geometry, making it particularly suitable for single-view depth estimation:

  • Works out-of-the-box: Pretrained on billions of images, generalizes to waste scenes without fine-tuning

  • Dense depth maps: Produces metrically consistent depth fields from single images

  • Segmentation awareness: Attention maps inherently separate objects and material clusters

Volume Computation Framework

The pipeline converts single RGB images into metric volume estimates through these steps:

Step 1: Pixel-to-Metric Scaling

For an image of resolution (Wpx, Hpx) and known physical ROI dimensions (Wm, Hm):

Apx = (Wm × Hm) / (Wpx × Hpx) [m²/pixel]

Step 2: Depth Map and Ground Reference

DINOv3 predicts dense depth z(x,y). The ground depth is estimated as the 99.9th percentile:

zg = percentile(z, 99.9)

Step 3: Height Map Conversion

Per-pixel height above ground:

h(x,y) = zg − scale × z(x,y)

Step 4: Volume Integration

Total volume within the ROI:

V = Σ h'(x,y) × Apx × M(x,y)

where M(x,y) is a binary mask restricting integration to valid regions.

Conclusion and Future Work

Summary

This thesis developed two complementary frameworks for waste management:

  1. Forecasting: Cross-country analysis revealed systematic relationships between development and waste generation. India is in a rapid-transformation phase with high urbanization elasticity, while China shows early decoupling and mature economies exhibit near-zero waste elasticity.

  2. Volume Estimation: A single-view depth-based system successfully translates 2D imagery into consistent volumetric estimates under controlled geometry, validating low-cost deployment feasibility.

Limitations

  • Forecasting depends on national-level statistics that may not capture local heterogeneity

  • Volume estimation assumes planar surface and fixed camera pose

  • Reflective/translucent materials can cause localized depth errors

Future Directions

  • Extend forecasting to include circular economy indicators and nonlinear ML models

  • Develop in-house calibration dataset with ground-truth volumes

  • Integrate semantic segmentation for per-material volume breakdown

  • Optimize for real-time deployment on edge devices

  • Closed-loop integration: Forecasted quantities set operational thresholds; observed volumes validate and refine forecasts in near real-time
