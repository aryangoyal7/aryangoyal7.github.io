Controllable Lung Nodule Synthesis

Shreshtha Singh†, Aryan Goyal†, Ashish Mittal†, Manoj Tadepalli, Piyush Kumar, Preetham Putha

A diffusion-based framework with characteristic-specific LoRA adapters for generating controllable, realistic lung nodules on chest X-rays. We introduce orthogonality-constrained adapter merging for stable multi-characteristic synthesis.

The Data Scarcity Problem

Early detection of lung cancer through chest X-rays (CXRs) is critical for patient outcomes. Deep learning-based Computer-Assisted Diagnosis (CAD) systems have shown promise, but their performance depends heavily on training data that captures the full diversity of lung nodule appearances.

The challenge? Lung nodules vary dramatically in their radiological characteristics—size, shape, texture, boundary morphology, visibility. Assembling datasets that adequately represent this diversity is expensive and often impractical. Rare but clinically important variants (like subtle nodules or those with specific calcification patterns) are particularly underrepresented.

Our Goal: Build a synthetic data generation framework that can produce realistic nodules with controllable characteristics—enabling targeted augmentation of underrepresented nodule types to improve CAD performance.

Understanding Nodule Characteristics

Radiologists describe lung nodules using several key characteristics. Our framework learns to control each of these independently:

Texture

Homogeneous: Uniform density throughout the nodule—appears as a consistent gray level.

Inhomogeneous: Uneven density with mixed bright and dark regions, often indicating complex internal structure.

Boundary Morphology

Regular: Smooth, well-defined margins that are clearly separated from surrounding lung tissue.

Irregular: Spiculated, lobulated, or poorly defined margins—often associated with malignancy.

Calcification

Bright, high-intensity regions caused by calcium deposits within the nodule. Certain calcification patterns (central, laminated, popcorn) are strongly associated with benign lesions.

Subtlety

Low-contrast nodules that blend with surrounding lung tissue, making them difficult to detect. These are often missed by both humans and CAD systems, making them particularly valuable for training.

Method Overview

Our framework combines a mask-conditioned Diffusion Transformer backbone with characteristic-specific Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) modules. The key insight is that different characteristics can be learned as independent "directions" in the model's parameter space, which can then be composed for multi-characteristic synthesis.

Complete framework: Mask-conditioned diffusion backbone with characteristic-specific LoRA adapters and orthogonality-constrained merging.

Training Pipeline

Training proceeds in four stages:

  1. Large-scale CXR pretraining: Train the base diffusion model on a large corpus of chest X-rays to learn the general distribution of CXR appearance.

  2. Nodule patch finetuning: Fine-tune on nodule patches with binary mask conditioning. This enables control over nodule size and location—the mask specifies where the nodule should appear.

  3. Independent LoRA training: Train separate LoRA adapters for each characteristic (texture, boundary, calcification, subtlety). Each adapter learns to steer generation toward its target attribute.

  4. Joint training with orthogonality: Fine-tune adapters together with an orthogonality constraint to enable stable multi-characteristic composition.

Characteristic-Specific LoRA Adapters

Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) provides an efficient way to adapt large pretrained models by learning low-rank updates to the weight matrices. For a frozen backbone weight W₀, we add:

W = W₀ + α · A · B

where A ∈ ℝd×r and B ∈ ℝr×k with rank r ≪ min(d, k). The scaling factor α controls the strength of the adaptation.

We train separate LoRA modules for each characteristic:

  • Calcification adapter: Learns to add bright calcium deposits.

  • Border regularity adapter: Controls margin smoothness vs. spiculation.

  • Texture adapter: Controls density uniformity.

  • Subtlety adapter: Controls nodule visibility/contrast.

Subtlety Control via Continuous Scaling

For subtlety, we use radiologist-annotated scores s ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} and map them to LoRA scaling:

α(s) = 2 / (2 + s)

Lower α produces fainter, more subtle nodules; higher α increases visibility. This enables clinically meaningful difficulty control for training detection models.

The Multi-Characteristic Challenge

Here's where it gets interesting. Nodule characteristics are not mutually exclusive. A real nodule might be:

  • Irregular-shaped AND calcified

  • Regular-shaped AND homogeneous

  • Subtle AND inhomogeneous

To generate such combinations, we need to merge multiple LoRA adapters. The naive approach is simple linear combination:

W = W₀ + α₁·A₁·B₁ + α₂·A₂·B₂

Why Naive Merging Fails

Unfortunately, simply adding LoRA updates doesn't work well. We observed:

  • One characteristic being suppressed by another

  • Synthetic-looking or nonsensical outputs

  • Unpredictable interactions between adapters

Root Cause: LoRA weights are highly sparse (60–70% near zero), meaning a small subset of parameters drives most perceptual changes. When adapters share these critical parameters, they interfere destructively.

We identified two key issues:

  1. Overlapping attention regions: Multiple adapters try to modify the same spatial locations. (Hard to fix—inherent to the task.)

  2. Non-orthogonal parameter spaces: Adapter updates are correlated, causing interference. (Fixable!)

Orthogonality-Constrained LoRA Merging

Our key contribution is an orthogonality loss that encourages adapters to operate in decoupled parameter subspaces:

L_orth = ‖W₁ · W₂ᵀ‖²_F

This Frobenius norm penalty pushes the adapter weight matrices to be orthogonal, meaning:

  • Decoupled parameter subspaces: Each characteristic controls different parts of the weight space.

  • Stable multi-attribute composition: Characteristics combine predictably.

  • Reduced spatial competition: Less interference during generation.

Layer-wise Frobenius norm comparison: cross-trained adapters (with orthogonality) vs. separately trained adapters. Lower norms indicate better decoupling.

Radiologist Evaluation

Task 1: Realism

50 pulmonary nodules (25 real + 25 synthetic from our base diffusion model) were shown to 3 expert radiologists in a blinded study:

• 90% of real nodules were correctly identified as real
• 80% of synthetic nodules were labeled as real (fooling the radiologists!)

Task 2: Controllability

For each characteristic, 10 nodules were generated using characteristic-specific LoRA adapters. Three radiologists verified whether the intended trait was present:

Nodule Characteristic Radiologist Agreement
Calcification80%

Conclusion

We presented a diffusion-based framework for controllable lung nodule synthesis with several key contributions:

  • Characteristic-specific LoRA adapters that independently control texture, boundary, calcification, and subtlety.

  • Orthogonality-constrained merging that enables stable multi-characteristic synthesis.

  • Demonstrated downstream benefit: synthetic augmentation improves nodule detection across multiple benchmarks.

  • Radiologist-validated realism: 80% of synthetic nodules fooled expert radiologists.

This work opens the door to targeted data augmentation—generating exactly the nodule types that are underrepresented in existing datasets to improve CAD system robustness.
